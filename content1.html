Mystery Misdiagnosis

The conversation surrounding twice-exceptional students—those who posses both high intelligence and learning disabilities—has long been plagued by a debate within educational circles. Some argue that due to the nature of twice-exceptional students accurate diagnosis can be all but impossible and at first glance this might make sense because by nature children with a high IQ will show similar characteristics to children with learning disabilities in a classroom where they aren’t challenged. However, a nuanced study in 2020 has revealed the root problem isn’t with twice-exceptional students but rather in the lack of specialized training among educators. Historically, the rhetoric surrounding twice-exceptional students has been focused on how similarly gifted and challenged students behave in classrooms rather than on the specific interactions that occur when they mix. In a classroom environment where a twice-exceptional or just plain gifted student isn’t sufficiently challenged, they will tend to exhibit similar behaviors (Bishop, 2020) which can complicate the diagnostic process. However, if we turn our focus away from the classroom and toward a testing environment we start to see where these two factions diverge. Twice-Exceptional students all show common characteristics in a testing environment which include knowing what questions they’ll get right very quickly but still taking a longer than average time on the assessment as a whole due to “thinking themselves in circles” on questions they have little to no chance at getting correct (Bishop, 2020). As a twice-exceptional student myself I can definitely confirm this is correct, all throughout middle school and the start of high school I had extended time on tests because I would spend an enormous amount of time on problems I had no idea how to do but as I’ve grown up and come to terms with the fact that I know what I know and not more than that I usually finish tests really quick- like first in a 300 person lecture hall to get up which is terrifying. Yes, I did eventually figure out how I learn without a special program but it took my until college and I still struggle with getting work done. As you’ll see in my article on future pathways if I had this knowledge since grade school I could have developed study skills that work for me much earlier. This proves twice-exceptional students can be relatively easily diagnosed given the learning specialist has received proper training in identifying these specific traits which is unfortunately not very common today. By simply adding this to curriculum's in education programs we can tackle what has been a massive barrier in getting twice-exceptional students the education they need.
